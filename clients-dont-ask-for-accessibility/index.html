99% of my clients don't ask for an accessible website, they just get one.

Accessibility still seems to be one of those things most designers and developers don’t want to think about. WAI Aria is a W3C Reccomendation since March of this year, but most websites I see don’t use Landmark roles, let alone Live Regions. And somehow, many developers don’t even care about semantic HTML anymore.

I’m not sure if it’s just lazyness or that designers and developers see accessibility as an annoying constraint, but I guess it’s a combination of both. Just like testing a website in Internet Explorer is an annoyance, so is testing it with a screen reader.

For some reason making websites accessible is still seen as something you do for that one blind guy, but designing an accessible website means you design it for everybody. That includes your grandma who needs her reading glasses, a co-worker who broke his arm and your colorblind girlfriend, but also that person who tries to access you site on hotel wifi. That means readable text, keyboard navigation, underlined links and a site that works when Javascript fails to load.

Although I have worked on several government websites, which are obliged to comply with the Dutch Web Guidelines (WCAG 2.0), most of the accessibility work I do is for projects where it’s not one of the requirements. Most of my clients don’t even know. Or care, to be honest.

I guess for many designers this lack of care translates to not having to care about it themselves. Most clients don’t give a damn if I use ARIA Roles or not or if choose to make something a <button> or an <a>, so I see this as a opportunity to do as much as I can to make it accessible.

Doesn’t it take more time?

Sure, sometimes. Sometimes you need to do some extra work to make sure an element is hoverable, or to do an extra check for color contrasts, but in my experience most issues can be resolved by just designing responsibly: using the correct HTML, picking a proper font size, making sure text zoom works, etc.

I know I lost years of my life debugging weird bugs in IE, sometimes things just take time. I consider it part of the job. At least making a client’s site more accessible is a much nicer and more gratifying part of the job.

Alright, you got me, now what?

These are some of the tools I use to make my work a little easier.

  • Accessibility Developer Tools (Chrome plugin), checks things like color contrast;
  • VoiceOver (Mac) is built in to OS X and a great way to see how your site is read by a screen reader;
  • HTML_CodeSniffer (Bookmarklet), a nice bookmarklet to check different conformance levels;
  • Revenge.css (Bookmarklet), this bookmarklet will make it painfully clear where your site needs improvement.

Once you include these tools into your workflow, you’ll see there are many quick-wins. Just start picking the low-hanging fruit and you’ll soon be making better websites without thinking about it. So please, designers and developers, I ask you to stop making a distinction between accessibility projects and regular projects. If you make every website as accessible as possible, we all benefit.
